7 Questions to Ask Before
Hiring an Agentic AI
Consultant

Augenti Research — March 2025 — 9 min read

The AI consulting market has exploded. Every generalist IT firm, strategy consultancy, and former software developer now claims agentic AI expertise. Most of them don't have it. Here's how to tell the difference — fast.

Why This Matters More Than You Think

Choosing the wrong partner for an agentic AI implementation doesn't just waste money. It can set your organisation back 12–18 months, damage internal confidence in AI adoption, and leave you with a system that doesn't work — or worse, one that works incorrectly without anyone realising.

The agentic AI implementation market in Australia is nascent. That creates genuine opportunity for early movers, but it also creates significant risk from underprepared vendors. The seven questions below are the ones we'd ask if we were hiring us.

"Every generalist IT firm and strategy consultancy now claims agentic AI expertise. Most of them don't have it. The questions you ask in the first meeting are your primary filter."

The 7 Questions

Q1 Can you show me a live agentic AI system you've built and deployed — not a demo, an actual production deployment?

This is the most important question. Agentic AI has a lot of vendors who can demo a convincing prototype. Building something that runs reliably in a production environment, with real data, under real business conditions, is categorically harder.

Ask to speak to the client. Ask what broke during deployment and how they fixed it. Ask what the agent does when it hits an edge case it hasn't seen before. The answer to these questions will tell you immediately whether you're talking to someone who's actually built this, or someone who's built a demo.

✓ Good sign

Specific system, named client (with permission), detailed war stories about what went wrong and how it was resolved.

✗ Red flag

Vague references to "several enterprise deployments," NDAs invoked to avoid specifics, demo-only evidence.

Q2 How do you handle failure modes? What happens when the agent takes a wrong action?

Agentic AI systems take real actions in the world — updating records, sending communications, processing transactions. A well-designed agent doesn't just succeed gracefully; it fails gracefully too.

Any experienced agentic AI practitioner should be able to speak in detail about rollback mechanisms, human-in-the-loop checkpoints, confidence thresholds, audit logging, and incident response procedures. If failure modes aren't part of their design thinking, they haven't built anything serious.

✓ Good sign

Detailed answers about specific failure scenarios, how they're detected, how they're contained, and how systems recover.

✗ Red flag

Focus only on capabilities and upside, minimal discussion of risk, generic answers about "testing."

Q3 What's your position on vendor selection — and do you have financial relationships with any AI vendors?

The agentic AI vendor landscape is developing rapidly, and the right platform choice depends heavily on your specific use case, existing technology stack, data environment, and risk tolerance. There is no universally correct answer — which means any consultant who recommends the same platform for every client is either not independent or not qualified.

Ask directly whether they receive referral fees, reseller margins, or other commercial benefits from vendor recommendations. A good partner discloses this clearly and explains how they manage the conflict. An excellent partner is genuinely vendor-agnostic and can explain exactly why they'd recommend different platforms for different scenarios.

✓ Good sign

Clear disclosure of any commercial relationships, evidence of having recommended different vendors for different clients, structured evaluation criteria.

✗ Red flag

Same vendor recommendation regardless of context, evasive answers about commercial relationships, excessive enthusiasm for one platform.

Q4 How do you handle Australian data sovereignty and privacy requirements?

Agentic AI systems access, process, and often store sensitive business data. For Australian businesses, this creates specific obligations under the Privacy Act 1988, industry-specific regulations (APRA, ASIC, TGA depending on your sector), and increasingly, organisational policies about data leaving Australian shores.

A qualified agentic AI partner should understand these requirements deeply — not hand them off with a "talk to your legal team." They should be able to describe specific architectural patterns for keeping sensitive data on-shore, approaches for minimising data exposure to foundation model providers, and how they've navigated these requirements in prior deployments.

✓ Good sign

Detailed knowledge of Australian privacy law, specific technical approaches to data sovereignty, prior experience with regulated industries.

✗ Red flag

Vague reassurances, suggestion that privacy is solely a legal/compliance matter, no discussion of technical architecture implications.

Q5 What does success look like at 30, 90, and 180 days — and how do you measure it?

Agentic AI projects that lack clear, measurable success criteria tend to drift — consumed by scope creep, integration complexity, or the perpetual pursuit of "just one more feature." Good partners front-load the measurement framework.

You want to hear specific metrics: processing time, error rates, cost per transaction, hours reclaimed, throughput volume. You also want to hear about the measurement infrastructure — how will those metrics actually be tracked, who owns them, and what happens if they're not hitting targets at the 90-day checkpoint?

✓ Good sign

Named KPIs with target ranges, defined measurement methodology, clear escalation protocol if targets aren't met.

✗ Red flag

Qualitative success language only ("improved efficiency"), no discussion of measurement methodology, vague timelines.

Q6 Who specifically will work on our project — and what's their actual experience with agentic AI?

This is the "bait and switch" question. In consulting, it's common for senior practitioners to win the work and junior staff to deliver it. For agentic AI — where genuine expertise is rare and the stakes of getting it wrong are high — this matters enormously.

Ask for the names and CVs of the specific people who will work on your project. Ask each of them what agentic AI systems they've personally built. Ask the engagement lead how much time they'll spend on your project each week. If you're getting polished seniors in the sales process and unknown juniors in the delivery team, that's a problem.

✓ Good sign

Named team members, specific projects they've worked on, transparent time allocation, senior practitioners hands-on in delivery.

✗ Red flag

Vague "team of experts," evasion on who specifically will do the work, senior staff "overseeing" a team you haven't met.

Q7 What's your exit strategy — how do we own this when you're done?

The goal of any good agentic AI implementation isn't perpetual consultant dependency — it's a system your team can operate, iterate, and scale independently. The best partners build towards their own irrelevance on the operational side, while remaining valuable for the next wave of capability building.

Ask for the handover plan on day one. What documentation will exist? What training will your team receive? What does the support model look like post-launch? Who owns the code and agent configurations? A partner who can't answer these questions clearly is building something your organisation will struggle to maintain.

✓ Good sign

Detailed handover framework, team training built into the project, your organisation owns all IP, clear post-launch support model.

✗ Red flag

Dependency baked into the commercial model, vague handover plans, proprietary tools that require ongoing vendor access.

The Bonus Test

Beyond the seven questions, there's one informal test that's surprisingly reliable: ask them to explain a technical concept in plain language.

Try: "Can you explain how a ReAct agent works without using any technical jargon?" or "What's the difference between tool use and function calling, in a way my CFO would understand?"

The ability to translate complex technical concepts into clear business language is a hallmark of practitioners who truly understand what they're doing. People who've absorbed information but haven't built anything real tend to struggle with this. It's a simple, fast, and revealing test.
